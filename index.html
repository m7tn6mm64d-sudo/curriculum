The Expertise Curriculum — overview

What this is, and what it is not

This is the answer to one question: if you asked someone who has already achieved mastery in health-system AI leadership how to build a strong expertise foundation in six months — and complete it in twelve — what would they tell you to read, watch, and understand, in what order? It is a mentor's curriculum: primarily passive (read, watch, listen, reflect), organized by knowledge domain, with every resource chosen because independent experts converge on it — not because it filled a slot.

It is not a task tracker. Your career execution plan — the hands-on deployment projects, the internal seat proposal, the job-search mechanics — lives in a separate tool and this curriculum never assigns its work. Where a concept here touches your live systems, you'll find "In practice" reflections: ideas to hold in mind at your next committee meeting or vendor call, never deliverables. There are no checkboxes to fall behind on. If you do nothing but read this curriculum on the couch, it is doing its job.

The two-layer design

RAPID (the 6-month core, now → ~January 2027). What a credible expert must have absorbed by the time your job search goes active: the mechanistic literacy to never be hand-waved, the governance and evaluation canon that is the seat's actual subject matter, the finance grammar of the CFO's table, and the field's map — its people, organizations, and cautionary tales. Everything tagged RAPID across the modules sums to roughly the passive half of your 8–12 weekly hours, leaving the rest for your execution plan.

FULL (the 12-month completion, → June 2027). Depth and second passes: the technical reports behind the explainers, the papers behind the summaries, optional hands-on excursions, and the frontier material that separates "well-read" from "the person others ask." FULL items assume their RAPID counterparts; nothing in FULL is load-bearing for the January gate.

Pause points, and the running-behind rule

Natural pause points sit at end of August (A done — a clean seam before B), end of November (C and G done, D underway), and mid-January (the RAPID gate). Life can interrupt at any of these without structural damage; interrupting mid-module costs more re-entry tax.

Running behind? Here is the spine. The 10-item spine in overview.md (~50–60h, all passive) is the guaranteed core: Wachter, the Karpathy deep dive, the CHAIO founding document, FURM, RUAIH + CHAI, the Epic sepsis arc, the NAM code, HFMA Module I, the PHTI ambient report, and SMART/FHIR literacy. If a month collapses, do only the spine items that month would have covered and let everything else slide right. The spine complete by mid-January is a pass; the spine incomplete is the only true "behind." Nothing in FULL can ever be behind — it fills the container it's given.

Three habits carry the whole year. Commute audio — Wachter in July, the Karpathy talks (audio-tolerable on second pass), the podcast episodes in I and D, the prequel in May; if your commute averages 30 minutes, that's ~4h/month you never feel. Medicare proposes its first standardized payment lane for clinical-AI software

CMS's CY2027 OPPS proposed rule (issued July 2; comments due Aug 31) creates an interim pathway for "Software as a Medical Service" (SaMS) — CMS's rename of algorithm-driven diagnostic software, formerly "SaaS." It designates 36 HCPCS codes as SaMS, moves the 21 separately-paid ones out of clinical APCs into New Technology APCs at roughly CY2026 rates, and adds a new status indicator "O1" (separate payment, no multiple-procedure discount). Named examples span CT-FFR, retinal-image disease detection, ECG-based risk scoring, quantitative brain MRI, and AI prostate-biopsy mapping. CMS calls it an interim "holding pen" pending a durable valuation method for 2028 and flags SaMS acquisition/licensing arrangements as a program-integrity risk. Why this matters to you: This is the reimbursement half of the clinical-AI story your governance/validation work usually leaves out. Map your health system's deployed diagnostic-AI tools against the 36-code SaMS list — build-vs-buy and revenue math both change once a diagnostic algorithm is separately billable. Carry the O1 / New-Tech-APC framing to your committee before the Aug 31 comment window; it pairs cleanly with last week's UpDoc note — FDA clearance and Medicare payment for clinical AI are now advancing in parallel. A1. First contact: what a language model actually is

What & why. A large language model (LLM) is a program that, given text, repeatedly predicts the most plausible next token (a word-piece — the units the model actually reads and writes). Everything you've watched your stewards do is that one operation, at scale. This unit gives you the two canonical first-hours in the field — the entry point every expert list agrees on. What to understand.

Resources.

  • "Training" and "inference" (using the trained model to answer) are different events with different economics.
  • The model has no database inside — its "knowledge" is a lossy compression of its training text.

A2. The visual mental model: transformers and attention

What & why. The transformer is the architecture — the wiring diagram — of every modern LLM, and attention is its core trick: each token looks at the other tokens in the input and decides which ones matter for predicting what comes next. You need the picture, not the equations; this is what stops "the model attends to context" from being a vendor slogan. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Words become embeddings — lists of numbers where similar meanings sit near each other.
  • Attention lets "the mass in the right upper lobe" connect to a pronoun forty words later — and is also why long inputs dilute focus.
  • Layers stack: early layers handle grammar-like structure, later ones something closer to meaning; nobody fully knows what individual weights do (that honest uncertainty matters in governance).

A3. The full training story — and why models hallucinate

What & why. This is the module's centerpiece. Pretraining (predict-the-next-token over an internet-scale corpus) makes a base model; supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (training further on human-written example conversations) makes it an assistant; RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback (humans rank outputs; the model is tuned toward preferred ones) polishes behavior. Each stage explains a family of behaviors you see daily — including hallucination: fluent, confident output that is factually wrong. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Which behaviors come from which stage — base models complete text; assistants were taught to follow instructions; the "personality" is RLHF.
  • Hallucination as incentive, not defect: training and benchmarks reward confident guessing over abstention — the same reason test-takers guess on multiple choice.
  • Why "we fine-tuned it for healthcare" tells you almost nothing until you know which stage, on what data, evaluated how.

A4. Tokens: how the model reads, and why clinical text breaks

What & why. Tokenization is how text gets chopped into the pieces the model actually sees — and medical vocabulary gets chopped badly ("erythromycin" becomes 4–5 arbitrary shards). Many "weird" model behaviors on ED notes — mangled drug names, miscounted values, brittleness to typos — are tokenization failures, not reasoning failures. Knowing the difference changes how you triage errors. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The model never sees letters or words — only token IDs; anything rare gets fragmented.
  • Numbers, units, abbreviations, and typos are exactly where fragmentation is worst — i.e., clinical text is a tokenization stress test.
  • This is why models "can't spell," struggle with character-level tasks, and sometimes flip on trivially rephrased inputs.

A5. Inference: temperature, context windows, and what the bill pays for

What & why. Inference is running the trained model — the thing you pay for per million tokens. Three concepts here do disproportionate governance work: temperature (the randomness dial — low = deterministic and repeatable, high = varied), the context window (the fixed token budget the model can consider at once; everything outside it doesn't exist), and per-token economics (why output tokens cost ~4–5× input, and why prices fall ~10×/year). What to understand.

Resources.

  • Why judgment systems like yours should run near temperature 0 — reproducibility is a validation property.
  • "Lost in the middle": models attend best to the start and end of long inputs; a bigger window ≠ reliable recall.
  • The one chart to internalize: cost per token at constant quality has fallen ~10×/year — which reframes every "AI is too expensive" budget conversation.

A6. The three levers: prompting vs retrieval vs fine-tuning

What & why. Every capability conversation reduces to three levers. Prompting shapes behavior through the input. Retrieval (RAG — retrieval-augmented generation) fetches relevant documents at question-time and pastes them into the context window so the model answers from them. Fine-tuning continues training on your own examples so behavior moves into the weights. Leaders who can't tell these apart approve the wrong projects. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The one-liner: prompting touches the input, RAG touches the context, fine-tuning touches the model.
  • The decision 2×2: context problems → retrieval; behavior problems → fine-tuning; prompting is always the first move — and the methods stack, they don't compete.
  • Why most organizations shouldn't fine-tune — and what that says about vendors who lead with "our fine-tuned clinical model."

Why this domain matters for your target. Most enterprise AI never earns its keep — the loudest 2025 finding put the failure rate of generative-AI pilots at 95%, and while that number deserves the skepticism it gets (unit B1 teaches you exactly why), the direction is not in dispute: the graveyard between pilot and P&L (profit and loss — the income statement where a project either shows up or doesn't) is where health-system AI programs go to die. The practitioner canon is blunt about the cause: unsuccessful LLM products almost always share "a failure to create robust evaluation systems", and Chip Huyen — whose book you read in Module A — calls evaluation "the biggest bottleneck to AI adoption." Meanwhile the seat you're aiming at is being redefined around exactly this: Epic now ships seismometer, an open-source local-validation toolkit, precisely because every Epic shop consuming Epic-native agents will need someone who can say whether they still work here. That someone is the role you're building.

What you bring / what's assumed. You already run production LLM systems — your stewards trace charts, render verdicts, and get validated against physician gold labels with kappa/AC1. That means you've been doing LLMOps by instinct, without its vocabulary. This module's job is to hand you the vocabulary and the canon so your instincts become citable discipline — the same move Module A7 made with AI Engineering. B2. Observability: what a trace records and why

B3. Evaluation harnesses and CI gates: how teams stop regressions

What & why. The module's centerpiece. An evaluation harness is a repeatable, scripted test battery — a set of cases with known-good answers, run automatically against the system, producing scores you can track over time. Wired into CI (continuous integration — the software practice of running all tests automatically on every change), it becomes a CI gate: a prompt edit or model swap that scores worse than the current version is blocked from shipping. This is how teams prevent a regression — a change that silently makes previously-correct behavior wrong. It is, per the field's most-cited essay, the thing failed LLM products didn't build. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Hamel Husain's three levels of evaluation: unit-test-like assertions (a unit test is, in software, a tiny automated check that one specific behavior still holds — e.g., "output must contain a verdict field" — and an assertion is the single pass/fail statement inside one; fast, cheap, every change), human + model evaluation (slower, richer — including LLM-as-judge, using one model to grade another's output, which must itself be validated against human judgment), and A/B tests in production (routing a random share of live traffic to the new version and comparing outcomes against the old — the RCT of software changes). Your validation program is a level-2 system; you've never had level 1 or 3 named for you.
  • What a golden set is (the curated cases-with-answers the harness runs against — your gold-label workbook, renamed) and why error analysis — actually reading the failures, one by one, and categorizing them — is the highest-yield activity in the whole discipline. B5. Designing for failure: fail-closed, incidents, rollback

What & why. Every system fails; the design question is what failure does. Fail-closed means that when a component can't do its job, the system stops or refuses — safety preserved, service sacrificed. Fail-open means it proceeds without the failed component — service preserved, safety check skipped. Neither is universally right; what's unacceptable is not having chosen. Around that dichotomy sits the operational kit: shadow mode (running a system silently alongside care, outputs recorded but not acted on), circuit breakers (automatic shutoffs when error signals cross a threshold), rollback (reverting to the last known-good version), and the incident-response vocabulary healthcare borrowed from site reliability engineering (SRE) — Google's discipline of running production systems, source of the blameless postmortem (an incident review that hunts causes in systems, not culprits in people; your M&M conference, renamed). Glossary additions

Blameless postmortem — an incident review, originating in site reliability engineering, that looks for causes in systems and processes rather than culprits in people — on the theory that blame teaches people to hide failures and systems to repeat them. The direct analog of a well-run M&M conference.

Circuit breaker — an automatic shutoff: when a deployed system's error signals cross a predefined threshold, it stops acting (or drops to shadow mode) without waiting for a human to notice. Named after the electrical device that does the same job.

CI gate — an evaluation harness wired into continuous integration so that any change (prompt edit, model swap, config change) is automatically scored before shipping, and blocked if it performs worse than the current version. The mechanism that stops regressions.

Error analysis — the practice of reading a system's failures one by one and categorizing them into patterns, rather than only computing aggregate scores. The field considers it the highest-yield activity in evaluation; it is what you do after each steward validation round when you sort disagreements into causes.

Fail-closed — a design in which a system that can't complete its safety-relevant work stops or refuses rather than proceeding. Preserves safety at the cost of availability; leaves an audit trail that says "refused — check unavailable."

Fail-open — the opposite design: when a component fails, the system proceeds without it. Preserves service at the cost of the skipped check; the audit trail shows the check simply never ran.

Gateway — the single controlled doorway all model traffic passes through on its way to and from providers — where logging, spend limits, access control, and failover live. When B5's reading says "AI gateway," this is the component it means.

Golden set — the curated collection of test cases with known-correct answers that an evaluation harness runs against. Your gold-label validation workbook is a golden set by another name.

LLM-as-judge — using one language model to grade another's outputs at scale. Cheap and fast, but the judge is itself a model that can err — so it must be validated against human judgment before its scores are trusted (the "who validates the validators?" problem, which inter-rater methodology like yours answers).

Model-health report — a recurring, committee-readable report on a deployed model: performance over time, subgroup/fairness breakdowns, input-distribution checks, usage. The operational artifact that turns "is it still working?" from a vibe into a governance decision.

Observation — in tracing vocabulary, one step inside a trace: a single model call, retrieval, or tool use. Traces are made of observations; scores can attach to either.

OpenTelemetry (OTel) — the vendor-neutral open standard for emitting telemetry (traces, metrics, logs) from software, with "semantic conventions" that standardize what things are called. Its GenAI conventions extend this to LLM data, so instrumentation can outlive any particular tool vendor.

Pilot purgatory — the common fate of enterprise AI projects: a pilot that demos well but never converts into a production system with an owner, a budget line, and measured outcomes. The condition the famous "95% fail" statistic gestures at, however imprecisely.

Regression — a change that makes previously correct behavior wrong: a prompt tweak that fixes one case and silently breaks five others. The failure mode CI gates exist to catch.

Rollback — reverting a deployed system to the last known-good version when a change or incident degrades it. Only possible if versions are tracked and the procedure is rehearsed — which is why "can we roll back?" is a governance question, not just a technical one.

Score — in tracing vocabulary, a quality judgment attached to a trace or observation after the fact — by a human annotator, a rule, or an LLM judge. The hook where validation data (like adjudicated steward verdicts) enters the observability record.

Shadow mode — running an AI system silently alongside real operations: it sees real inputs and produces real outputs, but nothing acts on them. Used to validate before go-live and as a safe place to park a system during an incident instead of switching it fully off.

Site reliability engineering (SRE) — the discipline (codified at Google) of running production systems reliably: incident command, on-call practice, postmortems, error budgets. The source tradition for most of AI operations' failure vocabulary.

Vector store — the database that holds embeddings (the numeric representations of text from the seed glossary) so retrieval/RAG can find similar text fast. One of the standard boxes on any LLM app-stack map.

C1. Why local validation exists: the Epic sepsis arc

What & why. Every discipline has a founding story; clinical-AI evaluation's is the Epic Sepsis Model. In 2021, Michigan Medicine ran an external validation (testing a model on data it wasn't developed on, at a site that didn't build it) of the most widely deployed sepsis prediction model in America and found an AUROC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve — the standard 0.5-to-1.0 score for how well a model separates positives from negatives) of 0.63 against the vendor's claimed 0.76–0.83, with 33% sensitivity and a 12% positive predictive value, while alerting on 18% of all hospitalizations. The 2026 sequel — a four-system prospective validation of the retrained model — found AUROC 0.82–0.92 but high inter-site variability, low PPV, and heavy alert burden. Together they establish the field's first principle: vendor claims are hypotheses, and local validation (testing on your own patients, in your own workflow, against your own labels) is how hypotheses get tested. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The 2021 numbers cold: AUC 0.63 vs claimed 0.76–0.83; sensitivity 33%; PPV 12%; alerts on 18% of hospitalizations. These are recited-in-interviews numbers.
  • Why the story didn't end in 2021: Epic retrained, recommended local training, and the 2026 multicenter study partially redeemed the model — the mature lesson is "local validation is non-optional," not "vendor AI is bad."
  • Inter-site AUROC spread (0.82–0.92 across four systems) as the punchline: the same tool is a different tool at every site.

C2. Agreement and gold standards: formalizing what you already do

What & why. Every evaluation reduces to comparing outputs against a reference standard (synonym: gold standard) produced by human adjudication — structured expert review that assigns the "true" label. You already run this machinery; this unit gives you its formal names and its two classic failure modes. Incorporation bias is when the reference standard is contaminated by the thing being tested — the adjudicator saw the AI's output, or labeled from the same note the model read, so agreement is inflated by construction. And the kappa-vs-AC1 choice you already make has a name in the literature: the kappa paradox — Cohen's kappa collapses toward zero under extreme prevalence even when raw agreement is high, which is why Gwet's AC1 (an alternative chance-corrected agreement coefficient that stays stable under skewed prevalence) exists. What to understand.

Resources.

C3. Calibration: the metric your workbook doesn't have yet

What & why. Discrimination (can the model rank positives above negatives — what AUROC measures) and calibration (do its probability outputs mean what they say — when it says 80%, does the event happen 80% of the time?) are independent properties, and a tool can be excellent at one while dangerously wrong at the other. Van Calster's hierarchy is the field's organizing frame: mean calibration (right on average) → weak (calibration intercept and slope — two summary numbers testing systematic over/under-prediction and over/under-confidence) → moderate (a smoothed observed-vs-predicted curve is right everywhere along its range) → strong (right for every patient subtype — provably unattainable). The canonical position: moderate calibration is the target. This is the highest-yield genuinely-new material in the module. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The four rungs of the hierarchy and why strong calibration is "utopic" — plus the practical reporting minimum: intercept, slope, and a smoothed curve.
  • Why miscalibration causes clinical harm even with good AUROC: decisions happen at thresholds, and a model that's 15 points overconfident moves patients across the threshold wrongly — the "Achilles heel" argument.
  • The tooling story: calzone, an open-source calibration package from FDA's Division of Imaging, Diagnostics, and Software Reliability, listed in FDA's Catalog of Regulatory Science Tools — "we assess calibration with FDA's own regulatory-science tooling" is a committee sentence that does real work.
  • How this maps to stewards: your confidence scores and verdict distributions are calibration objects the moment you treat them as probabilities.

C4. Subgroup and fairness analysis: slicing without fooling yourself

What & why. An aggregate metric is an average, and averages hide victims. Subgroup analysis (recomputing performance within slices of the population — by site, demographic group, or documentation pattern) is how you find out who the model fails. The 2025 addition to this literature is fairness drift: subgroup performance can degrade over time while the aggregate metric holds steady, which means fairness is a monitoring problem, not a one-time audit. The methodological danger is the mirror image: slice small enough and you'll always find a "disparity" that is pure noise — hence pre-registered slices and minimum-n rules. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The three slice families worth pre-registering: care pathway/site; age/sex/race-ethnicity/language; and documentation characteristics (note length, template use) — that third family is specific to language-model tools and mostly absent from the fairness literature.
  • Minimum-n rules: report a slice only at ≥~100 cases and ≥~20 events; below that, aggregate across quarters or report "insufficient n" — with bootstrap confidence intervals (resampling your own data thousands of times to get honest uncertainty bounds) as the display of record.
  • Fairness drift as the frame: monitor subgroup deltas in both discrimination and calibration over time, because AUROC-parity alone misses systematic over/under-estimation within a group.
  • Pre-registration beats fishing: slices chosen before looking are findings; slices found after looking are hypotheses.

C5. Sample size and honest claims: what n buys you

What & why. The cheapest credibility in evaluation science is refusing to overclaim. The rule of 3 says: observe zero errors in n cases, and the 95% upper confidence bound on the true error rate is approximately 3/n — so your existing n≈150 clean-case threshold licenses exactly the claim "error rate under 2%," and nothing stronger. That's not a limitation of your program; it's the definition of what your program measured — and saying so precisely is the tell of a methodologist. This unit generalizes: Wilson intervals (a binomial confidence-interval formula that behaves well at small n and extreme proportions, unlike the naive textbook formula) for any observed error count, and Riley's framework for sizing a validation study in advance to the confidence-interval widths you need. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Rule of 3, derived once so you own it (Hanley & Lippman-Hand, JAMA 1983): the probability of zero events in n trials at true rate p is (1−p)ⁿ; set it to 0.05 and solve — p ≈ 3/n.
  • Wilson over Wald at small n; and the general posture: size the study to the CI width of the claim, not to a significance test.
  • Riley's gravity for prediction-model validation: roughly 100–200 events (not cases) for stable estimates of calibration and discrimination — which reframes "is n=150 enough?" as "150 of what, with how many events, for which claim?"
  • The habit of stating claims as intervals: "zero errors in 150 → under 2% at 95% confidence" — pre-sized, pre-registered, then reported verbatim.

C6. LLM-as-judge: validating the instrument that grades your instruments

What & why. At production scale, humans can't adjudicate everything — so the field increasingly uses an LLM-as-judge (a language model scoring another model's outputs against a rubric). The validity question is recursive and delicious: the judge is itself a measurement instrument, so it needs the same validation you'd demand of any rater. The literature's two anchor numbers, held together: an intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC — the standard agreement statistic for continuous or ordinal ratings, the sibling of your kappa) of ≈0.818 between judge and clinicians in the best published clinical case (Croxford, npj Digital Medicine 2025), and ≈0.47 in MedHELM's jury grading. Resources.

Glossary additions

Adjudication — structured expert review that assigns the reference ("true") label for each case in an evaluation, including prespecified rules for resolving disagreements between reviewers. The human machinery behind every gold standard.

AUROC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve) — the standard summary of a model's discrimination: the probability that a randomly chosen positive case is ranked above a randomly chosen negative one. 0.5 = coin flip, 1.0 = perfect ranking. Says nothing about whether the model's probabilities are honest (see calibration).

Bootstrap confidence interval — an uncertainty bound produced by resampling your own dataset (with replacement) thousands of times and recomputing the metric each time. The honest way to display uncertainty for small slices and nonstandard metrics.

Calibration — whether a model's probability outputs mean what they say: among cases where it says 80%, does the event happen about 80% of the time? Independent of discrimination; assessed by intercept, slope, and smoothed observed-vs-predicted curves. Van Calster's hierarchy (mean → weak → moderate → strong) sets "moderate" as the practical target.

Calibration intercept and slope — the two summary numbers of "weak" calibration: the intercept detects systematic over- or under-prediction overall; the slope detects over-confidence (slope < 1) or under-confidence (slope > 1) across the probability range.

Case-mix shift — differences in the patient population between sites or time periods. A model's AUROC can change across sites purely because the population spread changed, not because the model got worse — which is why multi-site reports include case-mix tables.

Discrimination — a model's ability to rank positive cases above negative ones (what AUROC measures), as distinct from calibration. A model can discriminate well and still be badly miscalibrated.

External validation — testing a model on data it was not developed on, typically at a different site or time period. Contrast with local validation.

Fairness drift — degradation of a model's performance within specific subgroups over time while the aggregate metric stays stable. The reason fairness is a monitoring problem, not a one-time audit.

Gold standard / reference standard — the adjudicated "true" answers an AI's outputs are scored against. Its trustworthiness (blinding, independence from the model's inputs) bounds the trustworthiness of the whole evaluation.

Gwet's AC1 — a chance-corrected agreement coefficient that remains stable under extreme prevalence, where Cohen's kappa paradoxically collapses toward zero despite high raw agreement. Reporting both, with prevalence, is the defensible practice.

Incorporation bias — contamination of the reference standard by the thing being tested: the adjudicator saw the AI's output, or the label was built from the same information the model reads. Inflates agreement by construction; prevented by blinded, independent adjudication.

Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) — the standard agreement statistic for continuous or ordinal ratings across raters (the sibling of kappa, which handles categories). The metric used to validate LLM judges against clinician raters.

Label leakage — a specific route to incorporation bias: the reference standard is derived from inputs the model also sees (e.g., labeling the outcome from the same note the LLM reads), so the evaluation partly tests the model against itself.

LLM-as-judge — using a language model to score another model's outputs against a rubric. A measurement instrument in its own right: it requires validation against human adjudication, anchored rubrics, jury designs, and standing spot-audits before its scores are trusted.

Jury (LLM evaluation) — using three or more judge models (or prompts), aggregated by majority or mean, instead of a single LLM judge — reducing dependence on one model's idiosyncrasies.

Local validation — testing an AI tool on your own patients, in your own workflow, against your own adjudicated labels — as opposed to accepting the developer's numbers. The Epic sepsis arc is the canonical argument for it; "recurring local validation" adds a standing cadence.

O/E ratio (observed-to-expected) — the simplest calibration summary: observed event count divided by the model's predicted (expected) count. 1.0 = calibrated on average; above/below = systematic under-/over-prediction.

Reporting guideline — a published, journal-enforced checklist of what a study must disclose (the CONSORT/TRIPOD family). TRIPOD-LLM (Nature Medicine 2025) is the member covering LLM studies in medicine.

Rule of 3 — if zero errors are observed in n cases, the 95% upper confidence bound on the true error rate is approximately 3/n. Zero errors in 150 cases licenses "error rate under 2%" — and nothing stronger.

Spot-audit — standing human re-adjudication of a random sample of an LLM judge's calls (plus all borderline scores) each cycle, sized by rule-of-3 logic. The mechanism that keeps a validated judge validated.

Subgroup analysis — recomputing performance metrics within pre-registered slices of the population (site, demographics, documentation characteristics), under minimum-n rules, to find who the model fails — because aggregate metrics are averages, and averages hide victims.

TRIPOD-LLM — the reporting guideline for studies of large language models in medicine (Nature Medicine 2025), extending the TRIPOD prediction-model family. Doubles as a scoring rubric for vendor evidence.

Wilson interval — a binomial confidence-interval formula that behaves well at small sample sizes and extreme proportions, unlike the naive (Wald) textbook formula. The default for error-rate claims in small evaluations.

D4. NIST AI RMF + the Generative AI Profile — and the Measure gap

What & why. What to understand.

D5. FDA: the device perimeter, PCCP, and the CDS boundary

What & why. The FDA regulates AI only when it legally counts as a medical device, and three texts define the perimeter. The 2022 clinical decision support (CDS) guidance interprets the four statutory criteria under which decision-support software is not a device — the boundary that (probably) keeps your stewards outside FDA jurisdiction. The predetermined change control plan (PCCP) guidance — finalized August 2025 — lets AI device-makers pre-authorize future model updates so changes ship without a new clearance. And a total-product-lifecycle guidance for AI devices exists in draft (January 2025) — still draft as of July 2026, so cite it as such.

What to understand.

  • The four non-device CDS criteria — the crucial one in practice: the clinician must be able to independently review the basis of the recommendation rather than relying on it. Automation and opacity are what pull software across the device line.
  • PCCP's three parts: description of modifications; modification protocol (data management, retraining, performance evaluation, update procedures); impact assessment. In effect: FDA now regulates the process for changing a model, not just its frozen snapshot.
  • The regulatory status ledger, precisely: CDS guidance final (2022); PCCP final (Aug 2025); lifecycle guidance still draft, on FDA's FY2026 "B-list" for finalization (FDA publishes an annual guidance agenda; the B-list is its second-priority tier — intended, but not guaranteed, for finalization that fiscal year).   • Halamka's rule for the whole unit: "FDA clearance is important but insufficient" — clearance says a device may be marketed, not that it works in your hospital (healthsystemCIO, Oct 2025).

D6. HTI-1 transparency and the state-law wave

What & why. The mandatory transparency layer. Federally: ONC/ASTP's HTI-1 rule created certification criterion §170.315(b)(11), requiring certified electronic health records to disclose, for every predictive decision support intervention (DSI) (the rule's term for any technology — explicitly including LLM-based tools — that learns from data to produce recommendations), 31 source attributes across nine categories: what it does, purpose, cautioned out-of-scope uses, development data, fairness, external validation, performance, maintenance, update schedules. D7. The NAM Code of Conduct and the ethics/equity frame

What & why. The National Academy of Medicine's AI Code of Conduct (May 2025) is the national ethics touchstone: six commitments and ten principles (engaged, safe, effective, equitable, efficient, accessible, transparent, accountable, secure, adaptive) that supply the shared adjectives of every governance meeting. Paired with it, one paper: Obermeyer's 2019 Science study — the field's canonical demonstration of algorithmic bias (systematic error that disadvantages a group, introduced not by malice but by design choices) — in which a widely used algorithm's choice of cost as the training label, as a proxy for illness, encoded racial bias at national scale, because less money is spent on Black patients at the same level of sickness.

What to understand.

  • The ten principles as vocabulary, not doctrine — when a committee member says "but is it equitable?", the NAM text is what gives that word operational content.
  • The Obermeyer mechanism precisely: the bias entered through label choice, upstream of any model — which means no amount of model-side fairness tuning could have fixed it. The lesson generalizes: ask what the label really measures.
  • Ethics frameworks converge on the same demand as the governance ones: bias is a measurement problem — subgroup performance, monitored over time — not a values statement in a slide deck. (Singh's version: ask vendors targeted harm questions about specific subgroups, not broad equity questions.)

D8. When it goes wrong: hazards, harm, liability, and FURM

What & why. The canon's final layer is the failure literature — what actually goes wrong, and how a mature organization decides what to deploy anyway. Glossary additions

Algorithmic bias — systematic error in an AI system that disadvantages a group, introduced by design choices (which label to predict, which data to train on) rather than malice. Canonical example: an algorithm predicting healthcare cost as a proxy for illness under-served Black patients, because less is spent on them at the same level of sickness.

Applied Model Card — the Coalition for Health AI's standardized version of a model card for health AI — an "AI nutrition label" with a public schema, whose fields deliberately align with HTI-1's 31 source attributes so one document can serve both.

Clinical decision support (CDS) — software that gives clinicians information or recommendations to inform care decisions. Legally significant because federal law exempts CDS from medical-device regulation only if it meets four criteria — chief among them that the clinician can independently review the basis for the recommendation.

Coalition for Health AI (CHAI) — a large industry–academic consortium writing the practical standards of health-AI governance: playbooks, the Applied Model Card schema, a public model-card registry. A convener and standard-setter, not a regulator; it co-developed the Joint Commission's AI guidance.

Confabulation — the National Institute of Standards and Technology's official term for hallucination: fluent, confidently delivered output that is factually wrong. Using this word in committee signals you've read the federal source documents.

FAVES — the HTI-1 rule's shorthand for what health AI should be: fair, appropriate, valid, effective, safe.

FURM assessment — Stanford Health Care's framework for deciding whether to deploy an AI system: is it Fair, Useful, Reliable — assessed via ethics review, workflow-grounded usefulness simulation, financial projection, and a monitoring plan, all before go-live. The field's reference example of deployment decision-making as a discipline.

Governance (AI governance) — the structures, policies, and accountability chains an organization uses to decide which AI systems to deploy, on what conditions, and how they are monitored afterward. In practice: a committee with authority, an intake and review process, documentation standards, escalation paths — "accountability structure, not paperwork."

HTI-1 / §170.315(b)(11) — the federal rule (Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability) whose certification criterion (b)(11) requires certified electronic health records to disclose 31 standardized "source attributes" about any predictive decision support they contain, plus a publicly linked risk-management summary. The de-facto documentation standard for health AI even beyond its legal reach.

Intervention Risk Management — HTI-1's companion requirement to the source attributes: a documented risk analysis of each predictive decision support intervention across eight dimensions (validity, reliability, robustness, fairness, intelligibility, safety, security, privacy), plus mitigation and governance, with summaries publicly hyperlinked.

Model card — a structured disclosure document for an AI model: what it does, its intended use and out-of-scope uses, what data built it, how it performs (including across subgroups), and how it is maintained. The AI equivalent of a drug label.

ONC/ASTP — the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, reorganized as the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy: the federal office that certifies electronic health record technology and authored the HTI-1 transparency rule.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) — the federal standards agency's voluntary framework for managing AI risk, organized into four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) and seven trustworthiness characteristics. Its 2024 Generative AI Profile adapts it to LLMs with twelve defined risks. The vocabulary hub other health-AI frameworks cross-reference.

Patient safety organization (PSO) — a federally recognized entity to which providers can report safety events under legal confidentiality protections, encouraging honest reporting. The Joint Commission's AI guidance routes de-identified AI safety events through PSO channels.

Predetermined change control plan (PCCP) — an FDA-authorized plan, submitted with an AI medical device, that pre-specifies what future model modifications the maker may ship without a new regulatory clearance: a description of planned modifications, a modification protocol (data handling, retraining, performance evaluation, update procedures), and an impact assessment.

Predictive decision support intervention (DSI) — the HTI-1 rule's term for technology that learns from data to produce recommendations or outputs used in decision-making — a definition that explicitly reaches LLM-based tools. Predictive DSIs in certified EHRs trigger the 31-attribute disclosure requirement.

Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare (RUAIH) — the Joint Commission/CHAI guidance (September 2025) on hospital AI governance — seven elements spanning governance structures, transparency, data protections, quality monitoring, safety-event reporting, bias assessment, and training — and the voluntary, organization-level certification built on it (launched June 2026), whose domain 4 covers monitoring, evaluating, and validating AI performance.

E1. Inside the EHR: where the data actually lives

What & why. An electronic health record (EHR) system like Epic is not one database — it is a relay of three, each shaped by a different job. Epic's live production database (Chronicles) is built for one thing: sub-second reads and writes while you're seeing patients. Analytics would crush it, so every night the data is copied out — an ETL (extract, transform, load) process — into a SQL-queryable reporting database (Clarity), and from there into a dimensional data warehouse (Caboodle) organized for dashboards and population queries. Everything in E5, and most of what your stewards read, sits somewhere on this relay. What to understand.

Resources.

E2. The interop story: HL7v2 → FHIR, and why it took thirty years

What & why. Interoperability — getting systems to exchange data they can actually use — has one indispensable narrator: Brendan Keeler, whose Health API Guy is the explainer the field itself names, and who appears in four units of this module. His core thesis organizes everything: healthcare standards succeeded or failed on market forces and implementer needs, never on technical merit. HL7v2 (Health Level Seven version 2, a 1980s-era pipe-delimited messaging format) still carries the overwhelming majority of hospital traffic; its designed successor (HL7 V3) failed outright; and FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) finally won by borrowing the plumbing of the modern web — data as small, standardized resources (Patient, Encounter, Observation…) fetched over ordinary web APIs (application programming interfaces). What to understand.

Resources.

  • Why v2 worked (cheap, flexible, everywhere) and why the same flexibility means every hospital's v2 is a local dialect requiring an interface engine — middleware that translates between systems — to reconcile.
  • What a FHIR resource actually is at reading level: a labeled JSON package for one clinical concept, retrievable by URL, the same request shape at any conformant hospital.
  • The coexistence reality: v2 runs the internal feeds (admits, labs, orders); FHIR serves the apps and the outside world. Both, for decades yet.

E4. The policy layer: information blocking, TEFCA, USCDI

What & why. The technology of E2–E3 moved because the law moved. Three instruments matter, and one plain sentence each: information blocking rules (from the 21st Century Cures Act) make it illegal for providers and vendors to unreasonably interfere with access to electronic health information — the reason your patients now see notes and labs instantly. TEFCA (the Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement) is the federal umbrella connecting health-information networks into one nationwide exchange fabric via designated QHINs (Qualified Health Information Networks). USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability) is the federally required minimum data set — the list of data classes every certified EHR must be able to exchange. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Information blocking flips the old default: sharing is presumed, withholding needs a named exception. A slow-walked records request is now a compliance event with penalties.
  • The network stack in one line: private networks (CommonWell, Carequality) grew first; TEFCA federates them; QHINs are the on-ramps; volume is already in the hundreds of millions of records.
  • USCDI is a floor, not a ceiling — and "is that element in USCDI?" is the practical question behind whether data your AI needs is portably available.

Glossary additions

Caboodle — Epic's dimensional data warehouse: a simplified, star-schema copy of clinical data (fact tables of events ringed by dimension tables of context) optimized for dashboards, registries, and population queries. Fed from Clarity; the layer BI tools usually sit on.

Chronicles — Epic's live production database, the one clinicians read and write during care. Built for speed and availability, not analytics — which is why reporting happens on downstream copies instead.

Clarity — Epic's SQL reporting database: a nightly-refreshed, heavily normalized relational copy of Chronicles where hospital analysts write queries. The middle stop between the live system and the warehouse.

Dashboard — a single screen of charts and numbers, refreshed from a warehouse, built for a specific decision-maker. Executive dashboards show few numbers, trends, and exceptions; analyst dashboards are dense and drillable.

Data warehouse — a database organized for analysis rather than transactions: data copied in from operational systems, restructured for fast aggregate queries across many patients or years. Caboodle is the Epic-shop instance.

Downtime procedures — a hospital's pre-planned paper-and-manual workflows for operating when the EHR or network is unavailable — order sheets, runners, phone results. Their existence is universal; whether they're tested is the executive question.

ETL (extract, transform, load) — the batch process that copies data from one system to another, reshaping it on the way (e.g., Chronicles → Clarity nightly). The reason analytic data is always hours-to-a-day old, and a place where meaning can silently change.

Electronic health record (EHR) — the software system of record for clinical care: documentation, orders, results, billing capture. "Epic-class" EHRs bundle the production database, the reporting layers, and the vendor's ecosystem rules into one commercial relationship.

HL7v2 — the 1980s-era healthcare messaging standard (pipe-delimited event messages: admits, orders, results) that still carries most hospital-internal data traffic. Cheap and universal, but locally dialectal — every interface needs custom mapping.

Information blocking — under the 21st Century Cures Act, the illegal practice of unreasonably interfering with access to or exchange of electronic health information. Flips the old default: sharing is presumed, and withholding requires a named regulatory exception.

Interface engine — middleware that receives, translates, and routes messages (mostly HL7v2) between a hospital's systems, reconciling each vendor's dialect. The unglamorous machinery most "integration" actually runs on.

MFA (multi-factor authentication) — requiring a second proof of identity (token, phone prompt) beyond a password. Its absence on one server was the sworn-testimony entry point for the Change Healthcare attack.

QHIN (Qualified Health Information Network) — a network designated under TEFCA as an on-ramp to nationwide exchange: health systems connect to a QHIN, and QHINs exchange with each other under a common legal agreement.

Ransomware — an attack that encrypts an organization's systems and data, demanding payment for restoration. In healthcare, functionally an operations and patient-safety event: measurable mortality increases for patients admitted during an attack.

Resource (FHIR) — FHIR's unit of data: a small, standardized package for one clinical concept (Patient, Encounter, Observation, MedicationRequest), fetched over web APIs with the same request shape at any conformant system.

SMART on FHIR — the standard (Substitutable Medical Applications, Reusable Technologies) for launching third-party apps inside an EHR with secure, scoped, auditable access to FHIR data. The doorway Apple Health Records and the app-gallery ecosystem walk through.

SQL / relational database — data stored as linked tables of rows and columns, queried with SQL, the universal query language; "normalized" means facts split across many small tables to avoid duplication — thorough but tedious to query, which is why Caboodle simplifies it.

Star schema — a warehouse design pattern: a central fact table (one row per event — an encounter, an order) surrounded by dimension tables (patient, provider, time, location). Simple to query fast, which is the point.

TEFCA (Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement) — the federal framework connecting health-information networks into one nationwide exchange fabric, with QHINs as the participating networks and a common legal agreement replacing point-to-point contracts.

USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability) — the federally defined minimum set of data classes and elements every certified EHR must be able to exchange. A floor that expands in numbered versions; the practical test of whether a data element is portably available.

F1. How hospitals get paid

What & why. For inpatient care, Medicare pays a single lump sum per admission based on the DRG (diagnosis-related group) — a code assigned from the diagnoses and procedures documented in the chart — regardless of what the stay actually cost. Around that core sit the payer mix (the revenue-weighted blend of Medicare, Medicaid, commercial insurance, and self-pay — commercial pays more and cross-subsidizes the rest), the tension between fee-for-service (paid per thing done) and value-based payment (paid for outcomes and cost control), and the two-midnight rule — Medicare's test for whether a patient is a payable inpatient admission or an observation stay paid at outpatient rates. This unit is the grammar for everything after it. What to understand.

Resources.

  • One admission → one DRG → one payment: why documented complications and comorbidities (CC/MCC codes) shift the payment tier, and why "documentation integrity" is a revenue function, not paperwork pedantry.
  • Payer mix as destiny: the same case pays very differently by payer, so any claim of ROI (return on investment) — a project's return expressed relative to its cost; gross counts the dollars coming in, net subtracts the program's full cost first — that isn't payer-mix-adjusted is fiction.
  • Fee-for-service vs value-based payment as the field's tectonic drift — most revenue is still activity-based, but every strategic conversation gestures at the shift.
  • The two-midnight rule as the fault line utilization review lives on: inpatient vs observation is a physician-expectation judgment with a large payment delta attached — exactly why chart-review AI congregates there.

F4. Capital decisions: reading a pro forma

What & why. When your AI program needs real money, it goes to a capital committee, and the document it arrives in is a pro forma — a forward-looking projection of the project's costs and returns. Committees rank projects with three tools: NPV (net present value) — future cash flows discounted to today's dollars, "fund it" meaning NPV > 0; IRR (internal rate of return) — the discount rate at which NPV hits zero, compared against the organization's hurdle rate (the minimum return it demands); and payback period — years until the project returns its cost, a sanity check. This unit is reading comprehension, not spreadsheet-building: the goal is to sit in the committee meeting and follow every line. What to understand.

Resources.

  • Why a dollar next year is worth less than a dollar today (discounting) — and why committees rank on NPV, sanity-checking with IRR and payback, not the reverse.
  • What a sensitivity table is for: the finance office doesn't believe your point estimate any more than you believe a vendor's — it wants to see which assumptions break the case.
  • Contribution margin — revenue minus variable costs — as the honest value of "a freed bed-day is worth $X": fixed costs don't move when one patient does.
  • The anatomy of a committee-grade pro forma: volumes, payer-mix-adjusted revenue, cost ramp, sensitivity, payback line.

F6. Operations economics: throughput, boarding, capacity as revenue

Glossary additions

Boarding — admitted patients held in the ED because no inpatient bed is available; the visible symptom of broken throughput, now CMS-measured (see ECAT).

CC/MCC (complication or comorbidity / major CC) — secondary-diagnosis codes that move an admission into a higher-paying DRG tier; why documentation is a revenue function.

CFO (chief financial officer) — the executive who owns the financial statements, budget, and capital allocation; the person every funding case must ultimately persuade.

CHFP (Certified Healthcare Financial Professional) — HFMA's credential: the Business of Health Care course + exam (Module I) plus a case-based Operational Excellence exam (Module II).

Contribution margin — revenue minus variable costs; the honest per-unit value of a freed bed-day, because fixed costs don't change when one patient moves.

Denial — a payer's refusal to pay a submitted claim (wrong status, insufficient documentation, no prior authorization…). Denials avoided or overturned are among the hardest hard dollars in clinical AI.

DRG (diagnosis-related group) — the code that turns an inpatient admission into one lump-sum payment based on documented diagnoses and procedures, regardless of actual cost.

ECAT (Emergency Care Access and Timeliness) — the CMS electronic clinical quality measure of ED throughput: boarding >4h, ED length of stay >8h, arrival-to-treatment-space >1h, left-without-being-seen. Voluntary 2027, mandatory 2028, 2.0-percentage-point payment-update cut for non-reporters.

Fee-for-service — payment per service delivered: more activity, more revenue. Still the dominant engine of US hospital payment.

Hard vs soft dollars — hard dollars are cash that provably arrives or stops leaving (denials, refilled bed-days, penalties avoided); soft dollars are valued time savings that only become cash if someone converts them. Finance offices fund the former.

HFMA (Healthcare Financial Management Association) — the professional society of hospital finance; source of the Business of Health Care course, the CHFP, and the physician-advisor ROI literature.

Hurdle rate — the minimum return an organization demands before funding a project; the bar an IRR is compared against.

IRR (internal rate of return) — the discount rate at which a project's NPV equals zero; clears if it beats the hurdle rate. Used as a cross-check on NPV.

LWBS (left without being seen) — ED patients who leave before evaluation; one of ECAT's four components.

NPV (net present value) — a project's future cash flows discounted to today's dollars, minus the investment. The committee's primary ranking number: fund if NPV > 0.

Observation status — an outpatient billing status for hospitalized patients not expected to stay across two midnights; paid at outpatient rates — the other side of every inpatient-status dispute.

Operating margin — operating income as a percentage of operating revenue; US hospitals run ~1–2%, which is why capital is scarce.

Payback period — years until a project returns its initial cost; the sanity check alongside NPV and IRR.

Payer mix — the revenue-weighted blend of Medicare, Medicaid, commercial, and self-pay. Commercial cross-subsidizes the rest; every credible ROI model is payer-mix-adjusted.

Physician advisor — a physician who reviews charts for admission status, medical necessity, and denial appeals — the human function chart-review AI most directly augments (HFMA benchmark: ~398% net ROI, ~$318/chart).

Pro forma — a forward-looking financial projection for a proposed project: volumes, revenue, costs, returns by year. What a capital committee actually reads.

Revenue cycle — the machinery converting care into cash: registration → documentation → coding → billing → collections → denial management.

ROI (return on investment) — a project's return expressed relative to its cost, usually as a percentage. Gross ROI counts the dollars a program brings in (or stops losing); net ROI subtracts the program's full cost first. Every credible claim states which it is and whose payer mix it assumes — the F5 physician-advisor benchmark (~398%) is a net figure.

Throughput — the rate patients move through hospital capacity; with fixed beds, the lever that turns capacity into revenue. Boarding is its failure mode.

Two-midnight rule — Medicare's status test: inpatient admission is generally payable when the physician expects the stay to cross two midnights; otherwise observation. The anchor of status disputes and utilization review.

Value-based payment — payment tied to outcomes, quality, or total cost of care rather than volume; the field's slow shift away from fee-for-service.

G2. Clinical decision support: the Five Rights, alert fatigue, and why most CDS fails

What & why. Clinical decision support (CDS) is any tool that delivers knowledge or patient-specific information to clinicians to improve decisions — from interruptive pop-ups to order sets to, yes, your stewards. It is the field's flagship intervention and its most-documented disappointment: decades of evidence show most drug-safety alerts are overridden, mostly rationally. The canon here is two short works — Bates' Ten Commandments and the Osheroff-derived CDS Five Rights (the right information, to the right person, in the right format, through the right channel, at the right time) — plus the alert fatigue literature: the desensitization that sets in when clinicians are interrupted too often with too little value, until the warnings that matter get dismissed with the ones that don't. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The Ten Commandments (2003) — speed matters, anticipate needs, fit the workflow, little things count, monitor and respond — and how many of them your platform's steward deployment quietly obeys or violates.
  • The Five Rights as a diagnosis checklist: nearly every failed CDS project failed on format, channel, or time — almost never on the information being wrong.
  • The core numbers: published override rates for medication alerts run roughly 49–96%, and the safety literature treats alert burden itself as a patient-safety hazard.
  • Why the field's honest conclusion is that interruptive alerts are the costliest channel and should be the last resort — which is precisely the design argument for asynchronous review agents like yours.

G3. Quality measurement: eCQMs, how measures are specified, and why they drive behavior

What & why. An eCQM (electronic clinical quality measure) is a quality measure specified precisely enough that a computer can calculate it from EHR data — defined populations, coded criteria, explicit logic — and reported to programs like CMS's, where money rides on it. Quality measurement is the closest thing American healthcare has to a steering wheel: hospitals reorganize around what is measured, because measures carry payment. You have a live worked example: the ED throughput eCQM you already work on — four components (admitted-patient boarding >4h; ED length of stay >8h; arrival-to-treatment-space >1h; left-without-being-seen), voluntary reporting from January 2027, mandatory in CY2028, with a 2.0-percentage-point payment-update cut for non-reporters. This unit teaches the machinery around those facts: where measures come from, what "specified" means, and why the specification is the policy. What to understand.

Resources.

  • The measure lifecycle — conceptualization → specification → testing → implementation → use — and why a measure takes years: every criterion must be defensible, codeable, and testable across thousands of hospitals.
  • What a specification actually contains: initial population, denominator, numerator, exclusions, and value sets (the maintained lists of codes — diagnoses, medications, results — that define each concept in computable form).
  • Why eCQMs made data quality a clinical issue: the measure only sees what was documented in structured fields, so documentation behavior becomes the measure's real input.
  • The behavioral economics: a measure with payment attached changes staffing, bed management, and executive attention long before it changes any clinical guideline — which is why measure literacy is executive literacy.

Glossary additions

Alert fatigue — the desensitization that develops when clinicians receive too many interruptive warnings of too little value; eventually important alerts get dismissed along with trivial ones. Documented as a patient-safety hazard in its own right.

BPA (BestPractice Advisory) — Epic's name for a configurable interruptive or passive alert; the generic concept is a CDS alert.

CDS Five Rights — the framework for diagnosing decision-support design: the right information, to the right person, in the right format, through the right channel, at the right time. Originating with Osheroff's implementer's guide and adopted into CMS/AHRQ guidance.

Classification (vs. terminology) — a code system built to aggregate events into buckets for billing and statistics (ICD-10-CM, CPT), as opposed to a terminology built to capture clinical meaning (SNOMED CT). Mapping between the two is where meaning leaks.

Clinical decision support (CDS) — any tool that delivers knowledge or patient-specific information to clinicians (or patients) to improve decisions: alerts, order sets, reference links, and increasingly AI review agents.

Clinical informatics — the discipline concerned with how information and computation are used to deliver care — the sociotechnical system of people, workflow, data, and decisions, not just the software. A board-certified physician subspecialty since 2013.

Configuration (EHR) — changing an EHR's behavior through the vendor's built-in settings, rules, templates, and tools, as opposed to writing custom code. Most of what an EHR "does" at a given hospital is configuration.

CPOE (computerized provider order entry) — ordering medications, labs, and procedures through the EHR rather than on paper; the substrate most medication-safety CDS attaches to.

CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) — the AMA-maintained code set for procedures and services, built for billing.

eCQM (electronic clinical quality measure) — a quality measure specified precisely enough to be computed from EHR data (defined populations, value sets, explicit logic) and reported electronically to programs where payment rides on it.

ICD-10-CM — the US diagnosis classification: every billed encounter carries ICD codes. Built for billing and statistics, not for expressing clinical nuance.

LOINC — the code system for laboratory tests and clinical observations — it names the question being asked (e.g., which assay); maintained by the Regenstrief Institute.

Measure lifecycle — the staged path a quality measure travels: conceptualization → specification → testing → implementation → use/maintenance. Why a measure takes years to arrive.

Order set — a curated bundle of orders for a clinical scenario (e.g., sepsis admission); one of the highest-value, lowest-annoyance forms of CDS.

Override rate — the fraction of alerts clinicians dismiss without taking the suggested action; published rates for drug-safety alerts run roughly 49–96%, mostly for defensible reasons.

Physician builder — a clinician certified in the EHR vendor's build tooling (alerts, order sets, documentation templates) and credentialed by their health system to make governed changes directly.

RxNorm — the NLM-maintained normalized vocabulary for medications, linking brand names, generics, strengths, and dose forms so systems can agree that two drug records are the same drug.

SAFER Guides — ONC's self-assessment guides for EHR safety (e.g., High Priority Practices); the shared vocabulary of EHR change-control and safety committees.

SNOMED CT — the comprehensive clinical terminology: a hierarchical system for expressing what is clinically true (findings, disorders, procedures) with far more nuance than billing codes; the usual language of problem lists.

Sociotechnical model (Sittig-Singh) — the eight-dimension framework for analyzing health IT in context: hardware/software, clinical content, human-computer interface, people, workflow, internal policies, external rules, and measurement/monitoring. The standard answer to why EHR change is slow.

Terminology (clinical) — a code system designed to capture clinical meaning at full fidelity (SNOMED CT, LOINC, RxNorm), as opposed to a billing classification. See classification.

Value set — the maintained list of codes (diagnoses, medications, results — drawn from the terminologies above) that defines a clinical concept inside a measure or rule in computable form; the atoms that eCQM specifications are built from.

H1. Why health-IT deployments fail: the classics, and the AI rerun

What & why. Before AI, there was computerized provider order entry (CPOE) — electronic ordering of medications and tests — and its deployment history is the field's cautionary scripture. The founding insight, from the 2004 Ash, Berg & Coiera paper, is that information systems don't just fail to help; they generate unintended consequences — new error types (fragmented displays, workflow mismatches, communication channels silently deleted) that emerge from the collision between the system's model of work and how clinical work actually happens. This is a sociotechnical claim: the unit of analysis is never the software alone, always software-plus-people-plus-workflow. The modern AI version has a name too — pilot purgatory, the now-well-documented pattern of health-AI pilots that demo well, validate adequately, and then never scale, because nobody solved the workflow, governance, and adoption problems that were the actual problem.

What to understand.

  • The Ash/Berg/Coiera taxonomy: errors of entering and retrieving information, and errors of communication and coordination — both caused by the system, both invisible in a demo.
  • Why "the technology worked" and "the deployment worked" are independent claims — the CPOE literature includes a famous case (Han 2005) where a technically successful go-live coincided with increased mortality.
  • Pilot purgatory as the same disease in new clothing: the binding constraints on 2025–26 health AI are adoption conditions, not model performance.
  • The leader's takeaway: deployment failure is usually a design failure that was knowable in advance — which is what the rest of this module teaches you to see in advance.

H2. Implementation science at reading level: what the frameworks actually offer

What & why. Implementation science is the academic discipline that studies why proven practices don't get adopted — born from the finding that evidence-based interventions take, famously, on the order of 17 years to reach routine care. It has produced dozens of frameworks; a leader needs exactly two at fluency and one at recognition. RE-AIM — Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation, Maintenance — is five questions to ask of any deployment (who did it actually touch? did it work here? did the units and clinicians take it up? was it delivered as designed? is it still working a year later?). CFIR — the Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research — is a structured checklist of the determinants that make implementations succeed or fail (the intervention's characteristics, the "inner setting" of the organization, the "outer setting" around it, the individuals involved, and the implementation process). This unit is one honest pass: the vocabulary, not the academia — enough to decode committee-speak and to ask framework-grade questions without pretending to be an implementation researcher.

What to understand.

  • The core distinction the whole field exists to make: efficacy (it works in a study) ≠ effectiveness (it works in practice) ≠ uptake (anyone actually uses it). Different gaps, different evidence, different fixes.
  • RE-AIM as a leader's audit instrument: most failed deployments score fine on Effectiveness and zero on Reach or Maintenance — and nobody was measuring those.
  • CFIR at recognition level: when a consultant says "inner setting" or "implementation climate," you know the construct and the checklist it comes from.
  • Why the 2024 Implementation Science position is a big deal: it applies this machinery to generative AI specifically — arguing your exact technology class needs translational discipline, not just validation.

H3. Trust and clinician adoption: the speed limit

What & why. Clinicians are the most instrumented early-warning system a deployment has, and the most burned. Alert fatigue — desensitization to safety warnings from sheer volume, with override rates in the 90%+ range — is the debt every new clinical AI inherits: your users have spent fifteen years learning that things that interrupt them are usually wrong. This unit is the adoption layer: why trust is built by solving problems clinicians already have (and destroyed by creating work), why physician champions — respected clinicians who advocate for and troubleshoot a change among their peers — work when mandates don't, and what a working Chief Health AI Officer's adoption doctrine actually sounds like.

What to understand.

  • The alert-fatigue numbers and mechanisms (tiering, specificity, human-factors design) — because "how is this different from another alert?" is the first honest question any clinician will ask your AI.
  • Singh's doctrine in his own words: problems first, tools second; avoid "work creation tools"; and frontline qualitative signal outranks dashboards — if a clinician says it isn't working, they're usually right.
  • Why champions work: adoption spreads through local credibility networks, not through training modules — the practical residue of diffusion of innovations research — the tradition (Rogers) on how new practices spread person-to-person through social networks rather than by decree.
  • Trust is asymmetric: built in months of reliable, workload-reducing behavior; spent in one high-profile miss handled badly. Design the miss-handling before the miss.

H5. The sociotechnical read of choices you already made

What & why. The capstone. The Sittig–Singh sociotechnical model describes any health-IT system across eight interacting dimensions — hardware/software; clinical content; the human-computer interface; people; workflow and communication; internal policies and culture; external rules and regulations; and system measurement and monitoring — and insists that design decisions in any one dimension ripple through the rest. Your platform embodies several such decisions you made on instinct: reviewer e-signature (a people + workflow decision that keeps accountable humans in the loop), fail-closed design — when the system errs or is uncertain, it defaults to the safe state of requiring human review rather than silently passing (a software + monitoring decision), and governance-as-process — treating oversight as a standing review cycle rather than a one-time approval (a policies + measurement decision). This unit gives those choices their names, their literature, and their defense — which is what turns "we did it this way" into expertise a committee can examine.

What to understand.

  • The eight dimensions, at fluency — because they are the most useful structured way to talk about any deployment, including in an interview.
  • Why the model insists on interaction effects: e-signature isn't just a safety feature, it's an adoption feature (clinicians trust systems that visibly defer to them) and a governance feature (it creates an audit trail) — one decision, three dimensions.
  • The 2026 leadership evidence: psychological safety — a climate where staff can report problems and admit confusion without penalty — as deployment infrastructure, and clinician-led initiatives outperforming technologist-led ones. Your profile is the finding.
  • How to narrate your own build in this vocabulary: not "I was careful" but "the system is fail-closed across the automation boundary, with human accountability preserved at the signature layer and drift surveillance owned by a standing process."

Glossary additions

Alert fatigue — desensitization to safety warnings caused by sheer volume; clinicians override the large majority of electronic alerts, and each ignored alert trains further ignoring. The adoption debt every new clinical AI inherits.

CFIR (Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research) — a structured checklist of the factors that determine whether an implementation succeeds: the intervention's characteristics, the organization's "inner setting," the "outer setting" around it, the individuals involved, and the implementation process itself.

Change management — the discipline of moving an organization from current practice to new practice: building the case, the coalition, the wins, and the permanence. The #1 screened competency in physician informatics-executive hiring.

CPOE (computerized provider order entry) — electronic ordering of medications, tests, and procedures; the first great health-IT deployment wave, whose failure literature (alert fatigue, unintended consequences) is the prior for AI rollouts.

Fail-closed — a design stance in which the system's default under error or uncertainty is the safe state (e.g., routing to human review) rather than silent continuation. Opposite: fail-open.

Implementation science — the academic field studying why proven practices don't get adopted in routine care, and what determines uptake. Source of frameworks like RE-AIM, CFIR, and NASSS.

NASSS (Non-adoption, Abandonment, Scale-up, Spread, Sustainability) — Greenhalgh's framework for why health technologies fail to spread: seven domains of complexity, with failure predicted when too many domains are complicated at once.

Physician champion — a respected clinician who advocates for, troubleshoots, and lends local credibility to a change among peers. Adoption spreads through credibility networks; champions are how.

Pilot purgatory — the pattern of health-AI pilots that demo and validate well but never scale, because workflow, governance, and adoption conditions were never solved. The modern rerun of the CPOE failure literature.

Psychological safety — an organizational climate in which staff can report problems, admit confusion, and challenge decisions without penalty. In the 2026 leadership literature, treated as deployment infrastructure: no safety, no frontline signal, no working feedback loop.

RE-AIM — five questions to ask of any deployment: Reach (who did it touch?), Effectiveness (did it work here?), Adoption (did units and clinicians take it up?), Implementation (was it delivered as designed?), Maintenance (is it still working a year later?).

Sociotechnical (system) — the view that a health-IT system is never software alone but software-plus-people-plus-workflow-plus-policy, and that failures emerge from interactions among these. The Sittig–Singh model names eight interacting dimensions.

Unintended consequences — in the health-IT literature, the new error types a system creates (entry/retrieval errors, broken communication channels, workflow mismatches) as distinct from the old errors it fails to prevent.

I3. The ambient-scribe story: the field's flagship case study

What & why. If a 2026–27 interview panel asks you exactly one landscape question, it will be about ambient documentation — AI that listens to the clinical encounter and drafts the note (the "AI scribe"). It earned that status by being the fastest-adopted technology in recent healthcare memory: by late 2025, 62% of surveyed provider organizations had it in use. It is also the field's best evidence lesson, because the results are genuinely mixed: burnout measurably drops, documentation time falls — yet the reference evaluation, from PHTI (the Peterson Health Technology Institute), an independent nonprofit that evaluates digital-health technologies, concluded the financial impact remains unclear, with no consistent throughput gain. Master this one story and you hold, in miniature, the field's whole grammar: adoption vs. evidence, soft vs. hard return, platform vs. point solution. What to understand.

Glossary (complete — covers all nine modules; 187 terms)

Plain-language definitions, alphabetized. A term used in any module is either defined where it first appears or here.

Adjudication — structured expert review that assigns the reference ("true") label for each case in an evaluation, including prespecified rules for resolving disagreements between reviewers. The human machinery behind every gold standard.

Agent — an AI system that doesn't just answer once but takes a sequence of actions (reading documents, calling tools, deciding next steps) toward a goal. Your steward workflows — staged chart-reading with rules and routing — are agents in this sense.

Alert fatigue — the desensitization that develops when clinicians receive too many interruptive warnings of too little value; eventually important alerts get dismissed along with trivial ones. Documented as a patient-safety hazard in its own right — and the adoption debt every new clinical AI inherits.

Algorithmic bias — systematic error in an AI system that disadvantages a group, introduced by design choices (which label to predict, which data to train on) rather than malice. Canonical example: an algorithm predicting healthcare cost as a proxy for illness under-served Black patients, because less is spent on them at the same level of sickness.

Ambient documentation (AI scribe) — AI that listens to the clinician–patient conversation and drafts the note automatically. The most-adopted clinical AI category of 2025–26.

AMDIS (Association of Medical Directors of Information Systems) — the peer community of CMIOs and physician informatics executives; runs the surveys and meetings where incumbents compare notes.

AMIA (American Medical Informatics Association) — the scholarly society for informatics: the main academic meetings, the clinical informatics board community, and the field's principal job bank.

Applied Model Card — the Coalition for Health AI's standardized version of a model card for health AI — an "AI nutrition label" with a public schema, whose fields deliberately align with HTI-1's 31 source attributes so one document can serve both.

Attention — the transformer's core mechanism: for each token, the model weighs which other tokens in the input matter for predicting what comes next. It's how "the mass" connects to a pronoun forty words later — and why very long inputs dilute focus.

AUROC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve) — the standard summary of a model's discrimination: the probability that a randomly chosen positive case is ranked above a randomly chosen negative one. 0.5 = coin flip, 1.0 = perfect ranking. Says nothing about whether the model's probabilities are honest (see calibration).

Base model — a model after pretraining only: it completes text but hasn't been taught to be an assistant. The helpful-chatbot behavior is added later (see SFT, RLHF).

Benchmark — a standardized test set used to score models (e.g., medical-QA benchmarks). Useful for comparison; notoriously gameable and rarely predictive of performance on your data — which is why local validation exists.

BI (business intelligence) — the tools and practice of turning organizational data into charts, dashboards, and reports for decision-makers (Tableau and Power BI are the two big products in hospitals).

Blameless postmortem — an incident review, originating in site reliability engineering, that looks for causes in systems and processes rather than culprits in people — on the theory that blame teaches people to hide failures and systems to repeat them. The direct analog of a well-run M&M conference.

Boarding — admitted patients held in the ED because no inpatient bed is available; the visible symptom of broken throughput, now CMS-measured (see ECAT).

Bootstrap confidence interval — an uncertainty bound produced by resampling your own dataset (with replacement) thousands of times and recomputing the metric each time. The honest way to display uncertainty for small slices and nonstandard metrics.

BPA (BestPractice Advisory) — Epic's name for a configurable interruptive or passive alert; the generic concept is a CDS alert.

Build vs. buy — the recurring leadership decision between building a capability in-house, licensing it, or waiting for the EHR vendor to ship it. Standing questions: roadmap overlap, evidence quality, monitoring cost.

Caboodle — Epic's dimensional data warehouse: a simplified, star-schema copy of clinical data (fact tables of events ringed by dimension tables of context) optimized for dashboards, registries, and population queries. Fed from Clarity; the layer BI tools usually sit on.

Calibration — whether a model's probability outputs mean what they say: among cases where it says 80%, does the event happen about 80% of the time? Independent of discrimination; assessed by intercept, slope, and smoothed observed-vs-predicted curves. Van Calster's hierarchy (mean → weak → moderate → strong) sets "moderate" as the practical target.

Calibration intercept and slope — the two summary numbers of "weak" calibration: the intercept detects systematic over- or under-prediction overall; the slope detects over-confidence (slope < 1) or under-confidence (slope > 1) across the probability range.

Case-mix shift — differences in the patient population between sites or time periods. A model's AUROC can change across sites purely because the population spread changed, not because the model got worse — which is why multi-site reports include case-mix tables.

CC/MCC (complication or comorbidity / major CC) — secondary-diagnosis codes that move an admission into a higher-paying DRG tier; why documentation is a revenue function.

CDI (clinical documentation integrity) — the hospital function ensuring documentation supports the billing codes submitted; a major AI target (better-documented charts capture more revenue) and a flashpoint for payer pushback on AI-assisted upcoding.

CDS Five Rights — the framework for diagnosing decision-support design: the right information, to the right person, in the right format, through the right channel, at the right time. Originating with Osheroff's implementer's guide and adopted into CMS/AHRQ guidance.

CFIR (Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research) — a structured checklist of the factors that determine whether an implementation succeeds: the intervention's characteristics, the organization's "inner setting," the "outer setting" around it, the individuals involved, and the implementation process itself.

CFO (chief financial officer) — the executive who owns the financial statements, budget, and capital allocation; the person every funding case must ultimately persuade.

CHAIO (Chief Health AI Officer) — the health-system executive accountable for AI strategy, governance, and portfolio. Emerged 2023–24; standalone mostly at the largest systems, elsewhere folded into the CMIO role.

Change management — the discipline of moving an organization from current practice to new practice: building the case, the coalition, the wins, and the permanence. The #1 screened competency in physician informatics-executive hiring.

CHFP (Certified Healthcare Financial Professional) — HFMA's credential: the Business of Health Care course + exam (Module I) plus a case-based Operational Excellence exam (Module II).

Chronicles — Epic's live production database, the one clinicians read and write during care. Built for speed and availability, not analytics — which is why reporting happens on downstream copies instead.

CI (continuous integration) — in software, the practice of automatically running a battery of tests every time something changes, so a change that breaks behavior gets caught before it ships. An "eval gate" applies this idea to prompts and models.

CI gate — an evaluation harness wired into continuous integration so that any change (prompt edit, model swap, config change) is automatically scored before shipping, and blocked if it performs worse than the current version. The mechanism that stops regressions.

Circuit breaker — an automatic shutoff: when a deployed system's error signals cross a predefined threshold, it stops acting (or drops to shadow mode) without waiting for a human to notice. Named after the electrical device that does the same job.

Clarity — Epic's SQL reporting database: a nightly-refreshed, heavily normalized relational copy of Chronicles where hospital analysts write queries. The middle stop between the live system and the warehouse.

Classification (vs. terminology) — a code system built to aggregate events into buckets for billing and statistics (ICD-10-CM, CPT), as opposed to a terminology built to capture clinical meaning (SNOMED CT). Mapping between the two is where meaning leaks.

Clinical decision support (CDS) — any tool that delivers knowledge or patient-specific information to clinicians (or patients) to improve decisions: alerts, order sets, reference links, and increasingly AI review agents. Legally significant because federal law exempts CDS from medical-device regulation only if it meets four criteria — chief among them that the clinician can independently review the basis for the recommendation.

Clinical informatics — the discipline concerned with how information and computation are used to deliver care — the sociotechnical system of people, workflow, data, and decisions, not just the software. A board-certified physician subspecialty since 2013.

CMIO (Chief Medical Information Officer) — the established physician-executive seat for clinical information systems; the modal home for AI accountability where no standalone AI officer exists.

Coalition for Health AI (CHAI) — a large industry–academic consortium writing the practical standards of health-AI governance: playbooks, the Applied Model Card schema, a public model-card registry. A convener and standard-setter, not a regulator; it co-developed the Joint Commission's AI guidance.

Confabulation — the National Institute of Standards and Technology's official term for hallucination: fluent, confidently delivered output that is factually wrong. Using this word in committee signals you've read the federal source documents.

Configuration (EHR) — changing an EHR's behavior through the vendor's built-in settings, rules, templates, and tools, as opposed to writing custom code. Most of what an EHR "does" at a given hospital is configuration.

Container / Docker — a way of packaging software with everything it needs so it runs identically on any machine. Docker Compose is a single config file that starts several cooperating containers at once. Relevance to you: self-hosted tools arrive as containers.

Context window — the fixed budget of tokens a model can consider at once. Everything outside it effectively doesn't exist for the model; and even inside it, recall is uneven (see "lost in the middle" in Module A5).

Contribution margin — revenue minus variable costs; the honest per-unit value of a freed bed-day, because fixed costs don't change when one patient moves.

CPOE (computerized provider order entry) — ordering medications, labs, and procedures through the EHR rather than on paper; the substrate most medication-safety CDS attaches to, and the first great health-IT deployment wave whose failure literature (alert fatigue, unintended consequences) is the prior for AI rollouts.

CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) — the AMA-maintained code set for procedures and services, built for billing.

Dashboard — a single screen of charts and numbers, refreshed from a warehouse, built for a specific decision-maker. Executive dashboards show few numbers, trends, and exceptions; analyst dashboards are dense and drillable.

Data warehouse — a database organized for analysis rather than transactions: data copied in from operational systems, restructured for fast aggregate queries across many patients or years. Caboodle is the Epic-shop instance.

Denial — a payer's refusal to pay a submitted claim (wrong status, insufficient documentation, no prior authorization…). Denials avoided or overturned are among the hardest hard dollars in clinical AI.

Discrimination — a model's ability to rank positive cases above negative ones (what AUROC measures), as distinct from calibration. A model can discriminate well and still be badly miscalibrated.

Downtime procedures — a hospital's pre-planned paper-and-manual workflows for operating when the EHR or network is unavailable — order sheets, runners, phone results. Their existence is universal; whether they're tested is the executive question.

DRG (diagnosis-related group) — the code that turns an inpatient admission into one lump-sum payment based on documented diagnoses and procedures, regardless of actual cost.

Drift — the slow degradation of a deployed model's performance as the world changes around it (new documentation patterns, new order sets, new populations). The reason monitoring exists.

ECAT (Emergency Care Access and Timeliness) — the CMS electronic clinical quality measure of ED throughput: boarding >4h, ED length of stay >8h, arrival-to-treatment-space >1h, left-without-being-seen. Voluntary 2027, mandatory 2028, 2.0-percentage-point payment-update cut for non-reporters.

eCQM (electronic clinical quality measure) — a quality measure specified precisely enough to be computed from EHR data (defined populations, value sets, explicit logic) and reported electronically to programs where payment rides on it.

Electronic health record (EHR) — the software system of record for clinical care: documentation, orders, results, billing capture. "Epic-class" EHRs bundle the production database, the reporting layers, and the vendor's ecosystem rules into one commercial relationship.

Embedding — the representation of a token (or document) as a list of numbers where similar meanings sit near each other. The geometry the model actually reasons in.

Error analysis — the practice of reading a system's failures one by one and categorizing them into patterns, rather than only computing aggregate scores. The field considers it the highest-yield activity in evaluation; it is what you do after each steward validation round when you sort disagreements into causes.

ETL (extract, transform, load) — the batch process that copies data from one system to another, reshaping it on the way (e.g., Chronicles → Clarity nightly). The reason analytic data is always hours-to-a-day old, and a place where meaning can silently change.

Eval / evaluation harness — a repeatable, scripted way of testing an AI system against a set of cases with known answers, producing scores you can track over time. The software equivalent of your validation workbook.

External validation — testing a model on data it was not developed on, typically at a different site or time period. Contrast with local validation.

Fail-closed — a design in which a system that can't complete its safety-relevant work stops or refuses rather than proceeding. Preserves safety at the cost of availability; leaves an audit trail that says "refused — check unavailable."

Fail-open — the opposite design: when a component fails, the system proceeds without it. Preserves service at the cost of the skipped check; the audit trail shows the check simply never ran.

Fairness drift — degradation of a model's performance within specific subgroups over time while the aggregate metric stays stable. The reason fairness is a monitoring problem, not a one-time audit.

FAVES — the HTI-1 rule's shorthand for what health AI should be: fair, appropriate, valid, effective, safe.

Fee-for-service — payment per service delivered: more activity, more revenue. Still the dominant engine of US hospital payment.

FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) — the modern standard for exchanging healthcare data as structured "resources" (Patient, Encounter, Observation…) over web APIs. The lingua franca of health-data portability.

Fine-tuning — continuing a model's training on your own examples so desired behavior moves into the weights themselves (vs. prompting, which shapes behavior through the input). See also LoRA.

Foundation model — a large pretrained model (an LLM or its multimodal cousin) used as the base layer for applications, rather than a model built for one narrow task. "Foundation" because you build on it.

Frontier model — one of the largest, most capable commercial models at a given moment (GPT-class, Claude-class, Gemini-class). Contrast with small open models you can run locally.

FURM assessment — Stanford Health Care's framework for deciding whether to deploy an AI system: is it Fair, Useful, Reliable — assessed via ethics review, workflow-grounded usefulness simulation, financial projection, and a monitoring plan, all before go-live. The field's reference example of deployment decision-making as a discipline.

Gateway — the single controlled doorway all model traffic passes through on its way to and from providers — where logging, spend limits, access control, and failover live. When B5's reading says "AI gateway," this is the component it means.

General-purpose technology — a technology (electricity, the internet, plausibly AI) whose payoff arrives only after years of complementary organizational redesign, not at adoption.

Gold standard / reference standard — the adjudicated "true" answers an AI's outputs are scored against. Its trustworthiness (blinding, independence from the model's inputs) bounds the trustworthiness of the whole evaluation.

Golden set — the curated collection of test cases with known-correct answers that an evaluation harness runs against. Your gold-label validation workbook is a golden set by another name.

Governance (AI governance) — the structures, policies, and accountability chains an organization uses to decide which AI systems to deploy, on what conditions, and how they are monitored afterward. In practice: a committee with authority, an intake and review process, documentation standards, escalation paths — "accountability structure, not paperwork."

Gwet's AC1 — a chance-corrected agreement coefficient that remains stable under extreme prevalence, where Cohen's kappa paradoxically collapses toward zero despite high raw agreement. Reporting both, with prevalence, is the defensible practice.

HAIP (Health AI Partnership) — a Duke-anchored collaborative publishing free, practitioner-facing guides on adopting AI safely; the field-manual counterpart to CHAI's standards.

Hallucination — fluent, confident output that is factually wrong. A property of how these systems are trained (guessing is rewarded over abstaining), not a vendor-specific bug.

Hard vs soft dollars — hard dollars are cash that provably arrives or stops leaving (denials, refilled bed-days, penalties avoided); soft dollars are valued time savings that only become cash if someone converts them. Finance offices fund the former.

HFMA (Healthcare Financial Management Association) — the professional society of hospital finance; source of the Business of Health Care course, the CHFP, and the physician-advisor ROI literature.

HIMSS (Healthcare Information and Management Systems Society) — the health-IT industry's trade organization; its annual conference is the field's commercial floor.

HL7v2 — the 1980s-era healthcare messaging standard (pipe-delimited event messages: admits, orders, results) that still carries most hospital-internal data traffic. Cheap and universal, but locally dialectal — every interface needs custom mapping.

HTI-1 / §170.315(b)(11) — the federal rule (Health Data, Technology, and Interoperability) whose certification criterion (b)(11) requires certified electronic health records to disclose 31 standardized "source attributes" about any predictive decision support they contain, plus a publicly linked risk-management summary. The de-facto documentation standard for health AI even beyond its legal reach.

Hurdle rate — the minimum return an organization demands before funding a project; the bar an IRR is compared against.

ICD-10-CM — the US diagnosis classification: every billed encounter carries ICD codes. Built for billing and statistics, not for expressing clinical nuance.

Implementation science — the academic field studying why proven practices don't get adopted in routine care, and what determines uptake. Source of frameworks like RE-AIM, CFIR, and NASSS.

Incorporation bias — contamination of the reference standard by the thing being tested: the adjudicator saw the AI's output, or the label was built from the same information the model reads. Inflates agreement by construction; prevented by blinded, independent adjudication.

Inference — running a trained model to get answers (as opposed to training it). What API bills charge for, priced per million tokens.

Information blocking — under the 21st Century Cures Act, the illegal practice of unreasonably interfering with access to or exchange of electronic health information. Flips the old default: sharing is presumed, and withholding requires a named regulatory exception.

Interface engine — middleware that receives, translates, and routes messages (mostly HL7v2) between a hospital's systems, reconciling each vendor's dialect. The unglamorous machinery most "integration" actually runs on.

Interoperability — the ability of different health-IT systems to exchange and actually use each other's data. The problem FHIR exists to solve.

Intervention Risk Management — HTI-1's companion requirement to the source attributes: a documented risk analysis of each predictive decision support intervention across eight dimensions (validity, reliability, robustness, fairness, intelligibility, safety, security, privacy), plus mitigation and governance, with summaries publicly hyperlinked.

Intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) — the standard agreement statistic for continuous or ordinal ratings across raters (the sibling of kappa, which handles categories). The metric used to validate LLM judges against clinician raters.

IRR (internal rate of return) — the discount rate at which a project's NPV equals zero; clears if it beats the hurdle rate. Used as a cross-check on NPV.

Jury (LLM evaluation) — using three or more judge models (or prompts), aggregated by majority or mean, instead of a single LLM judge — reducing dependence on one model's idiosyncrasies.

KLAS — the healthcare-IT ratings firm that scores vendors from structured customer interviews; "Best in KLAS" is the industry's most-advertised vendor credential.

Label leakage — a specific route to incorporation bias: the reference standard is derived from inputs the model also sees (e.g., labeling the outcome from the same note the LLM reads), so the evaluation partly tests the model against itself.

Large language model (LLM) — a program that, given text, repeatedly predicts a plausible next token — scaled up until that single operation produces fluent language, reasoning-like behavior, and everything you see in ChatGPT-class systems.

LLM-as-judge — using one language model to grade another's outputs at scale. Cheap and fast, but the judge is itself a measurement instrument that can err — so it requires validation against human adjudication, anchored rubrics, jury designs, and standing spot-audits before its scores are trusted (the "who validates the validators?" problem, which inter-rater methodology like yours answers).

LLMOps — the operational discipline of running LLM systems in production: tracing what they do, evaluating changes before they ship, monitoring for drift, alerting on failures. (The LLM-era rename of "MLOps.")

Local validation — testing an AI tool on your own patients, in your own workflow, against your own adjudicated labels — as opposed to accepting the developer's numbers. The Epic sepsis arc is the canonical argument for it; "recurring local validation" adds a standing cadence.

LOINC — the code system for laboratory tests and clinical observations — it names the question being asked (e.g., which assay); maintained by the Regenstrief Institute.

LoRA (low-rank adaptation) — the standard budget technique for fine-tuning: small "adapter" matrices are trained on top of the frozen model — about 1% of the parameters — instead of retraining the whole thing.

LWBS (left without being seen) — ED patients who leave before evaluation; one of ECAT's four components.

Measure lifecycle — the staged path a quality measure travels: conceptualization → specification → testing → implementation → use/maintenance. Why a measure takes years to arrive.

MFA (multi-factor authentication) — requiring a second proof of identity (token, phone prompt) beyond a password. Its absence on one server was the sworn-testimony entry point for the Change Healthcare attack.

Model card — a structured disclosure document for an AI model: what it does, its intended use and out-of-scope uses, what data built it, how it performs (including across subgroups), and how it is maintained. The AI equivalent of a drug label.

Model-health report — a recurring, committee-readable report on a deployed model: performance over time, subgroup/fairness breakdowns, input-distribution checks, usage. The operational artifact that turns "is it still working?" from a vibe into a governance decision.

NAM (National Academy of Medicine) — the national honorific body and convener; its 2025 AI Code of Conduct (six commitments, ten principles) supplies the shared ethical vocabulary of governance meetings.

NASSS (Non-adoption, Abandonment, Scale-up, Spread, Sustainability) — Greenhalgh's framework for why health technologies fail to spread: seven domains of complexity, with failure predicted when too many domains are complicated at once.

NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) — the federal standards agency's voluntary framework for managing AI risk, organized into four functions (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage) and seven trustworthiness characteristics. Its 2024 Generative AI Profile adapts it to LLMs with twelve defined risks. The vocabulary hub other health-AI frameworks cross-reference.

NPV (net present value) — a project's future cash flows discounted to today's dollars, minus the investment. The committee's primary ranking number: fund if NPV > 0.

Observability — the property of a system that lets you see what it actually did and why: logs, traces, dashboards. In LLM systems: recording every prompt, response, and decision so behavior can be audited later.

Observation — in tracing vocabulary, one step inside a trace: a single model call, retrieval, or tool use. Traces are made of observations; scores can attach to either.

Observation status — an outpatient billing status for hospitalized patients not expected to stay across two midnights; paid at outpatient rates — the other side of every inpatient-status dispute.

O/E ratio (observed-to-expected) — the simplest calibration summary: observed event count divided by the model's predicted (expected) count. 1.0 = calibrated on average; above/below = systematic under-/over-prediction.

ONC/ASTP — the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, reorganized as the Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy: the federal office that certifies electronic health record technology and authored the HTI-1 transparency rule.

Open weights — a model whose learned parameters are published for anyone to download and run (with license terms). Contrast with API-only frontier models.

OpenTelemetry (OTel) — the vendor-neutral open standard for emitting telemetry (traces, metrics, logs) from software, with "semantic conventions" that standardize what things are called. Its GenAI conventions extend this to LLM data, so instrumentation can outlive any particular tool vendor.

Operating margin — operating income as a percentage of operating revenue; US hospitals run ~1–2%, which is why capital is scarce.

Order set — a curated bundle of orders for a clinical scenario (e.g., sepsis admission); one of the highest-value, lowest-annoyance forms of CDS.

Override rate — the fraction of alerts clinicians dismiss without taking the suggested action; published rates for drug-safety alerts run roughly 49–96%, mostly for defensible reasons.

Parameter / weight — one of the billions of learned numbers inside a model. "A 4B model" = four billion parameters. The weights ARE the model; everything it "knows" is encoded in them.

Patient safety organization (PSO) — a federally recognized entity to which providers can report safety events under legal confidentiality protections, encouraging honest reporting. The Joint Commission's AI guidance routes de-identified AI safety events through PSO channels.

Payback period — years until a project returns its initial cost; the sanity check alongside NPV and IRR.

Payer mix — the revenue-weighted blend of Medicare, Medicaid, commercial, and self-pay. Commercial cross-subsidizes the rest; every credible ROI model is payer-mix-adjusted.

PHTI (Peterson Health Technology Institute) — an independent nonprofit that evaluates digital-health technologies against evidence of clinical and financial value; its ambient-scribe report is the category's reference verdict.

Physician advisor — a physician who reviews charts for admission status, medical necessity, and denial appeals — the human function chart-review AI most directly augments (HFMA benchmark: ~398% net ROI, ~$318/chart).

Physician builder — a clinician certified in the EHR vendor's build tooling (alerts, order sets, documentation templates) and credentialed by their health system to make governed changes directly.

Physician champion — a respected clinician who advocates for, troubleshoots, and lends local credibility to a change among peers. Adoption spreads through credibility networks; champions are how.

Pilot purgatory — the common fate of enterprise AI projects: a pilot that demos well but never converts into a production system with an owner, a budget line, and measured outcomes. The condition the famous "95% fail" statistic gestures at, however imprecisely.

Platform vs. point solution — a point solution solves one problem standalone; a platform (an EHR vendor, or a broadened startup) absorbs adjacent problems into one offering, often commoditizing the point solutions in its path.

Predetermined change control plan (PCCP) — an FDA-authorized plan, submitted with an AI medical device, that pre-specifies what future model modifications the maker may ship without a new regulatory clearance: a description of planned modifications, a modification protocol (data handling, retraining, performance evaluation, update procedures), and an impact assessment.

Predictive decision support intervention (DSI) — the HTI-1 rule's term for technology that learns from data to produce recommendations or outputs used in decision-making — a definition that explicitly reaches LLM-based tools. Predictive DSIs in certified EHRs trigger the 31-attribute disclosure requirement.

Pretraining — the first, enormous stage of training: predict-the-next-token over internet-scale text. Produces a base model.

Pro forma — a forward-looking financial projection for a proposed project: volumes, revenue, costs, returns by year. What a capital committee actually reads.

Productivity paradox — the historical pattern in which measured productivity fails to rise for years after a general-purpose technology is adopted, because workflows haven't yet been redesigned around it.

Prompt injection — an attack where text the model reads (a document, a chart note, a webpage) contains instructions that hijack its behavior. The security concept most relevant to systems that read free text written by others.

Prompting — shaping a model's behavior through instructions and examples in the input text. Your current expert-level craft.

Psychological safety — an organizational climate in which staff can report problems, admit confusion, and challenge decisions without penalty. In the 2026 leadership literature, treated as deployment infrastructure: no safety, no frontline signal, no working feedback loop.

QHIN (Qualified Health Information Network) — a network designated under TEFCA as an on-ramp to nationwide exchange: health systems connect to a QHIN, and QHINs exchange with each other under a common legal agreement.

Quantization — compressing a model's weights to lower precision (e.g., 4-bit) so it runs smaller and faster with modest quality loss. How big models fit on small machines.

RAG / retrieval (retrieval-augmented generation) — fetching relevant documents at question-time and pasting them into the context window so the model answers from them rather than from memory. Your platform's chart-extract assembly is hand-rolled RAG.

Ransomware — an attack that encrypts an organization's systems and data, demanding payment for restoration. In healthcare, functionally an operations and patient-safety event: measurable mortality increases for patients admitted during an attack.

RE-AIM — five questions to ask of any deployment: Reach (who did it touch?), Effectiveness (did it work here?), Adoption (did units and clinicians take it up?), Implementation (was it delivered as designed?), Maintenance (is it still working a year later?).

Regression — a change that makes previously correct behavior wrong: a prompt tweak that fixes one case and silently breaks five others. The failure mode CI gates exist to catch.

Reporting guideline — a published, journal-enforced checklist of what a study must disclose (the CONSORT/TRIPOD family). TRIPOD-LLM (Nature Medicine 2025) is the member covering LLM studies in medicine.

Resource (FHIR) — FHIR's unit of data: a small, standardized package for one clinical concept (Patient, Encounter, Observation, MedicationRequest), fetched over web APIs with the same request shape at any conformant system.

Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare (RUAIH) — the Joint Commission/CHAI guidance (September 2025) on hospital AI governance — seven elements spanning governance structures, transparency, data protections, quality monitoring, safety-event reporting, bias assessment, and training — and the voluntary, organization-level certification built on it (launched June 2026), whose domain 4 covers monitoring, evaluating, and validating AI performance.

Revenue cycle (RCM) — the machinery converting care into cash: registration → documentation → coding → billing → collections → denial management. A major AI vendor category because its outputs are dollar-denominated.

RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) — the polishing stage of training: humans rank candidate outputs, and the model is tuned toward the preferred ones. Where the "assistant personality" and much of the guessing-over-abstaining incentive comes from.

ROI (return on investment) — a project's return expressed relative to its cost, usually as a percentage. Gross ROI counts the dollars a program brings in (or stops losing); net ROI subtracts the program's full cost first. Every credible claim states which it is and whose payer mix it assumes — the F5 physician-advisor benchmark (~398%) is a net figure.

Rollback — reverting a deployed system to the last known-good version when a change or incident degrades it. Only possible if versions are tracked and the procedure is rehearsed — which is why "can we roll back?" is a governance question, not just a technical one.

Rule of 3 — if zero errors are observed in n cases, the 95% upper confidence bound on the true error rate is approximately 3/n. Zero errors in 150 cases licenses "error rate under 2%" — and nothing stronger.

RxNorm — the NLM-maintained normalized vocabulary for medications, linking brand names, generics, strengths, and dose forms so systems can agree that two drug records are the same drug.

SAFER Guides — ONC's self-assessment guides for EHR safety (e.g., High Priority Practices); the shared vocabulary of EHR change-control and safety committees.

Score — in tracing vocabulary, a quality judgment attached to a trace or observation after the fact — by a human annotator, a rule, or an LLM judge. The hook where validation data (like adjudicated steward verdicts) enters the observability record.

Self-hosting — running software on machines your organization controls rather than the vendor's cloud — the standard requirement when patient data (PHI) is involved.

SFT (supervised fine-tuning) — the stage after pretraining where the model is trained on human-written example conversations, turning a text-completer into an instruction-following assistant.

Shadow mode — running an AI system silently alongside real operations: it sees real inputs and produces real outputs, but nothing acts on them. Used to validate before go-live and as a safe place to park a system during an incident instead of switching it fully off.

Site reliability engineering (SRE) — the discipline (codified at Google) of running production systems reliably: incident command, on-call practice, postmortems, error budgets. The source tradition for most of AI operations' failure vocabulary.

SMART on FHIR — the standard (Substitutable Medical Applications, Reusable Technologies) for launching third-party apps inside an EHR with secure, scoped, auditable access to FHIR data. The doorway Apple Health Records and the app-gallery ecosystem walk through.

SNOMED CT — the comprehensive clinical terminology: a hierarchical system for expressing what is clinically true (findings, disorders, procedures) with far more nuance than billing codes; the usual language of problem lists.

Sociotechnical model (Sittig–Singh) — the view that a health-IT system is never software alone but software-plus-people-plus-workflow-plus-policy, and that failures emerge from interactions among these. The Sittig–Singh model names eight interacting dimensions: hardware/software, clinical content, human-computer interface, people, workflow, internal policies, external rules, and measurement/monitoring. The standard answer to why EHR change is slow.

Spot-audit — standing human re-adjudication of a random sample of an LLM judge's calls (plus all borderline scores) each cycle, sized by rule-of-3 logic. The mechanism that keeps a validated judge validated.

SQL / relational database — data stored as linked tables of rows and columns, queried with SQL, the universal query language; "normalized" means facts split across many small tables to avoid duplication — thorough but tedious to query, which is why Caboodle simplifies it.

Star schema — a warehouse design pattern: a central fact table (one row per event — an encounter, an order) surrounded by dimension tables (patient, provider, time, location). Simple to query fast, which is the point.

Subgroup analysis — recomputing performance metrics within pre-registered slices of the population (site, demographics, documentation characteristics), under minimum-n rules, to find who the model fails — because aggregate metrics are averages, and averages hide victims.

TCO (total cost of ownership) — a technology's full lifetime cost: license, implementation, training, and — the piece leaders forget with AI — post-deployment monitoring and model refresh.

TEFCA (Trusted Exchange Framework and Common Agreement) — the federal framework connecting health-information networks into one nationwide exchange fabric, with QHINs as the participating networks and a common legal agreement replacing point-to-point contracts.

Temperature — the randomness dial at inference: low (near 0) = deterministic, repeatable answers; high = varied, creative ones. Judgment systems like stewards run near zero because reproducibility is a validation property.

Terminology (clinical) — a code system designed to capture clinical meaning at full fidelity (SNOMED CT, LOINC, RxNorm), as opposed to a billing classification. See classification.

Throughput — the rate patients move through hospital capacity; with fixed beds, the lever that turns capacity into revenue. Boarding is its failure mode.

Token — the unit a model actually reads and writes: a word-piece, roughly ¾ of an English word on average. Costs, context windows, and many failure modes are all denominated in tokens.

Tokenization — how text is chopped into tokens. Medical vocabulary gets chopped badly (rare drug names fragment into arbitrary shards), which is behind many "weird" model behaviors on clinical text.

Trace — the recorded sequence of everything an AI system did in one run: inputs, intermediate steps, outputs, timing. The raw material of observability.

Transformer — the neural-network architecture behind every modern LLM; its distinctive feature is attention. "GPT" = generative pretrained transformer.

TRIPOD-LLM — the reporting guideline for studies of large language models in medicine (Nature Medicine 2025), extending the TRIPOD prediction-model family. Doubles as a scoring rubric for vendor evidence.

Two-midnight rule — Medicare's status test: inpatient admission is generally payable when the physician expects the stay to cross two midnights; otherwise observation. The anchor of status disputes and utilization review.

Unintended consequences — in the health-IT literature, the new error types a system creates (entry/retrieval errors, broken communication channels, workflow mismatches) as distinct from the old errors it fails to prevent.

USCDI (United States Core Data for Interoperability) — the federally defined minimum set of data classes and elements every certified EHR must be able to exchange. A floor that expands in numbered versions; the practical test of whether a data element is portably available.

Value-based payment — payment tied to outcomes, quality, or total cost of care rather than volume; the field's slow shift away from fee-for-service.

Value set — the maintained list of codes (diagnoses, medications, results — drawn from the terminologies above) that defines a clinical concept inside a measure or rule in computable form; the atoms that eCQM specifications are built from.

Vector store — the database that holds embeddings (the numeric representations of text from the seed glossary) so retrieval/RAG can find similar text fast. One of the standard boxes on any LLM app-stack map.

Wilson interval — a binomial confidence-interval formula that behaves well at small sample sizes and extreme proportions, unlike the naive (Wald) textbook formula. The default for error-rate claims in small evaluations.
